Review of The Strokes' The New Abnormal

A Well-balanced Formula

As a long time fan of the Strokes, I higly anticipated the release of this album. Their last album Comedown Machine, which was released in 2013, was met with mixed reviews, as it sharply departed from their previous sound in both promising and mediocre ways. For me, the first listen did not disappoint, and with each listen of the album I only grew to like it more.

In The New Abnormal, the band returns to their roots, echoing moods and sounds from Is This It? while injecting eighties elements that keep the revival on trend rather than stale. Most of "The Adults Are Talking" sounds like something right off of one of their first two albums, but the falsetto brings it back to their newer sound. Julian's falsetto actually makes an appearance on several of the tracks, notably "Eternal Summer" and "Why Are Sundays So Depressing." I personally welcome this vocal style from Julian, but I know it has also been met with criticism.

The general album composition reminds me of their last record. A light, middle-of-the-road song to start ("Tap Out" vs. "The Adults Are Talking"), hard-hitting bangers sandwiched in the middle ("One Way Trigger" & "50/50" vs. "Brooklyn Bridge to Chorus"), and finishing off with a somber track ("Call It Fate, Call It Karma" vs. "Ode To The Mets").

However, this is the first Strokes record that included synthesizers. Julian finally brought this element from his side and personal projects to The Strokes, and I think it is an excellent touch, particularly in "At The Door." Overall, the record deviates just enough from their usual music to not be jarring, but offers distinct and new flavors that add another layer of sweet nostalgia.

Track Highlight: "At The Door"

"I can't escape it / Never gonna make it / Out of this in time / I guess that's just fine"

The bare instrumentals coupled with Julian's unique voice make this my favorite track on the record. The choice to use only guitar and synth elevate this track and make the listener truly feel the helplessness and apathy the lyrics convey. The traditionally animated music video completes the "vintage" aesthetic.
